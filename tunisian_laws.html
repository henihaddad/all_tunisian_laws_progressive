Tunisia's legal system is predominantly civil law-based, derived from the French legal tradition with elements of Islamic law (particularly in personal status). The unified secular judiciary was established in 1956 upon abolition of Shari'a courts. The current constitution is the 2022 Constitution (adopted July 25, 2022 by referendum; published August 18, 2022), which supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions. Official legal texts are published in the Journal Officiel de la République Tunisienne (JORT).

  • Art. 40: Freedom to form political parties, unions, and associations is guaranteed

Tunisia's first post-independence constitution. Modeled on the French system, vested strong executive power in a president. Arabic declared national language. Presidents elected for 5-year terms. 2022 Constitution, Articles 116–120Constitutional Court

Established by: 2022 Constitution | Replaces: defunct Constitutional Council
The 2022 Constitution establishes a Constitutional Court replacing the defunct Constitutional Council. Composed of 9 members: 3 appointed by president, 3 elected by parliament, 3 elected by Supreme Judicial Council. 9-year non-renewable terms, staggered. Reviews constitutionality of laws before promulgation and settles conflicts of competence. Under the 2022 Constitution, the Constitutional Court cannot rule on the validity of the president's exceptional measures (Art. 96). Status as of June 2026: still not established — neither this court nor its 2014-Constitution predecessor has ever become operational; legal scholars and the Venice Commission describe Tunisia as having effectively gone without a functioning constitutional review body since 2011. The ARP's General Legislation Committee resumed hearings in February 2026 on Organic Law Proposal No. 100/2025, a private member's bill to actually create the Court under Chapter VI of the 2022 Constitution; as of mid-2026 the proposal remained under committee review with no vote scheduled.

Local Council Elections of December 24, 2023Seating of the National Council of Regions and Districts (CNRD)

First-round vote: December 24, 2023 | Second round: January 2024 | Implements: 2022 Constitution bicameral structure
Tunisia's first elections held entirely under the 2022 Constitution's "bottom-up" local governance model. Voters elected 2,155 members across 279 local councils for 5-year terms via a majoritarian two-round system (turnout: 11.7% round one, 12.53% round two — record lows). Each local council designates a member by lot to a regional (governorate) council; the 24 regional councils each elect a representative to one of 5 district councils; the district and regional councils together indirectly elect the 77-member National Council of Regions and Districts (CNRD), the upper chamber of parliament envisioned by the 2022 Constitution. The CNRD held its inaugural session in 2023, completing the bicameral legislature alongside the Assembly of People's Representatives. Critics, including IFES and the Carnegie Endowment, describe the multi-tiered indirect system as insulating the upper chamber from direct accountability.

👪 Personal Status Law

The Code of Personal Status (CPS) is Tunisia's most internationally distinctive statute — it abolishes polygamy and replaced unilateral repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce. It has been continuously in force since 1957.

Code of Personal Status (CPS) — Beylical Decree of August 13, 1956Majallat Al-Ahwal Al-Shakhsiyya

Enacted: August 13, 1956 (beylical decree under Lamine Bey) | In force: January 1, 1957 | Amended: 1959, 1966, 1981, 1993, 2017
Tunisia's landmark personal status code, principally drafted by President Habib Bourguiba. Abolished polygamy (criminal offense under Art. 18), replaced unilateral male repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce, set minimum marriage age. The most progressive family law in the Arab world. Covers marriage, divorce, filiation, adoption (recognition of kafala), inheritance, guardianship, and wills. Never suspended or repealed despite political pressures (2011–2013). The 2022 Constitution has not altered its operation.
Key provisions
  • Art. 3: Marriage requires consent of both spouses; minimum age 18 (with exceptions)
  • Art. 5: Marriage must be registered and conducted before a civil status officer
  • Art. 18: Polygamy prohibited and criminalized — up to 1 year imprisonment + fine for marrying while already married
  • Art. 29–37: Divorce only by court order; grounds include mutual consent, harm, abandonment
  • Art. 31: Either spouse may initiate divorce; court-ordered alimony mandatory
  • Art. 46–67: Filiation and parentage; paternity presumption
  • Art. 68–78: Legal guardianship (tutelle) of minors
  • Art. 85–199: Inheritance rules (miras) — blends civil and Islamic principles
  • Art. 154–165: Guardianship over persons under disability
  • 1993 amendment: mother gains equal custody rights; divorced mothers may become guardians
  • 2017 amendment: allowed Muslim women to marry non-Muslim men (circular, not codified)

Organic Law No. 2018-50 of October 23, 2018Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination

Adopted by the ARP: October 9, 2018 | Promulgated: October 23, 2018 | In force: 2018
Landmark statute making Tunisia the first country in the Middle East and North Africa to criminalize racial discrimination. Article 2 defines racial discrimination broadly as any distinction, exclusion, restriction, or preference based on race, color, descent, or national or ethnic origin that impairs the equal enjoyment of rights and freedoms, aligning domestic law with Tunisia's obligations under the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (ratified 1967). Criminalizes discriminatory acts, incitement to racial hatred, and public glorification of racial discrimination, with enhanced penalties for organized or repeated offenses; legal entities that promote racial discrimination face fines. Article 11 establishes a National Commission against Racial Discrimination, attached to the ministry responsible for human rights, tasked with data collection, monitoring, and policy proposals. Implementation note (2024–2026): civil society and international observers (EuroMed Rights, MERIP, Arab Reform Initiative) describe the law's practical enforcement as limited — few prosecutions have been brought under it directly — while Saadia Mosbah, president of the prominent anti-racism organization Mnemty, was instead prosecuted on unrelated money-laundering and "illicit enrichment" charges (May 2024 arrest; 8-year sentence upheld on appeal, June 2026), a case Amnesty International and other rights groups characterize as the weaponization of ordinary criminal law against a leading advocate for the rights this statute was meant to protect.

Law No. 2024-41 of August 2, 2024Reform of Bad-Cheque Rules (amending Commercial Code Arts. 410 et seq.)

Published in JORT No. 94: August 2, 2024 | Entered into force: February 2, 2025
Major reform of the bounced-cheque ("chèque sans provision") regime, widely described as a turning point given Tunisia's historically high rate of check-related criminal prosecutions. Shifts the system toward bank-side solvency screening, a centralized verification platform, and partial decriminalization for low-value cheques.
Key provisions
  • Banks must assess a customer's solvency before issuing a chequebook and set a global ceiling and validity period (max. 6 months) per chequebook based on financial capacity
  • Maximum face value capped at 30,000 TND per cheque leaf
  • Decriminalizes issuing a bad cheque for amounts ≤ 5,000 TND — recourse becomes a civil/payment-order matter unless the beneficiary initiates proceedings
  • Criminal liability retained for cheques exceeding the 5,000 TND threshold, fraud, or bad faith
  • Establishes the Central Electronic Cheque Platform (PECC), operated by the Central Bank of Tunisia, allowing real-time verification and reservation of available funds by beneficiaries

Draft Law No. 113/2020 (adopted by Council of Ministers, never promulgated)Consumer Credit & Household Over-indebtedness

Adopted by Council of Ministers: December 2020 | Status: Never promulgated; revived debate 2026
Tunisia has no dedicated statute defining household over-indebtedness or regulating consumer-credit affordability; a bill (referenced in secondary sources as "Loi n° 113/2020") addressing both was approved by the Council of Ministers in December 2020 but was never submitted to, or adopted by, parliament, leaving a persistent legal vacuum — no credit bureau, no over-indebtedness observatory, and no statutory debt-to-income caps. As of mid-2026 no such law had been enacted. Note: the "113/2020" bill number is drawn from secondary press/policy sources, not independently verified against a primary JORT or ARP record.

Law No. 2025-9 of May 21, 2025Employment Reform Law

Enacted: May 21, 2025 | Published: JORT May 23, 2025
Landmark reform overhauling Tunisia's employment framework. Three pillars: (1) Indefinite employment contracts (CDI) are the statutory default; CDDs (fixed-term contracts) permitted only in three circumstances: temporary workload increase, replacement of absent worker, and seasonal work; any non-compliant CDD is void and automatically converted to CDI with full seniority. Law No. 2004-63 of July 27, 2004 (organic reform bill pending, 2025–2026)Personal Data Protection

Enacted: July 27, 2004 | In force: 2004 | Amended: 2013 | Reform bill: introduced 2025, under ARP Rights & Freedoms Committee review as of 2026
Tunisia's data protection statute — one of the first in Africa. Creates the National Authority for Personal Data Protection (INPDP — Instance Nationale de Protection des Données Personnelles). Requires prior authorization from INPDP before processing sensitive personal data. Establishes data subject rights (access, rectification, deletion) and obligations on data controllers. The bill aligns Tunisian law with the EU GDPR and Council of Europe Convention 108+, addressing biometric data, facial recognition, and algorithmic profiling, and introducing GDPR-style turnover-based administrative fines. Adoption is partly aimed at securing EU "adequacy" status for cross-border data transfers. 2026–2030 National Development Plan (Bill)

Filed under Constitution Arts. 75 & 77 | ARP standing-committee examination: June 29–30, 2026 | Status: pending plenary vote as of July 1, 2026
The economic and social planning framework law for 2026–2030, formally deposited at the ARP as draft law n°42/2026 on June 17, 2026 following its adoption by the Council of Ministers. Structured as a single-article bill — the Assembly votes yes/no/abstain on the plan as a whole rather than amending individual provisions, per committee rapporteur Saber Jlassi — it was examined jointly by all Assembly of People's Representatives standing committees on June 29–30, 2026 but not yet put to a plenary vote. Committee discussion noted the prior five-year plan's execution rate stood at under 40%, a benchmark cited by critical deputies alongside the new plan's more ambitious targets. A June 30, 2026 joint hearing with Minister of Economy and Planning Samir Abdelhafidh preceded committee deliberations, followed by a second joint ARP audition of the Minister on July 1, 2026 to gather further committee input (Tunisian media noted the plan's limited treatment of the media/audiovisual sector as a specific point raised in that session, and a July 1, 2026 report noted several deputies voicing doubts about the feasibility of the plan's growth and investment targets, with the Finance Committee separately flagging concerns over the underlying economic and financial assumptions). The standing committees are compiling a consolidated synthesis report for submission to the ARP Bureau, which will then schedule the plenary session before the text proceeds to the Conseil National des Régions et des Districts under Art. 77.
