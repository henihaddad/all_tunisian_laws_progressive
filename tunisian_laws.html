Legal Family
Tunisia's legal system is predominantly civil law-based, derived from the French legal tradition with elements of Islamic law (particularly in personal status). The unified secular judiciary was established in 1956 upon abolition of Shari'a courts. The current constitution is the 2022 Constitution (adopted July 25, 2022 by referendum; published August 18, 2022), which supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions. Official legal texts are published in the Journal Officiel de la République Tunisienne (JORT).

👪 Personal Status Law

The Code of Personal Status (CPS) is Tunisia's most internationally distinctive statute — it abolishes polygamy and replaced unilateral repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce. It has been continuously in force since 1957.

Code of Personal Status (CPS) — Beylical Decree of August 13, 1956Majallat Al-Ahwal Al-Shakhsiyya

Enacted: August 13, 1956 (beylical decree under Lamine Bey) | In force: January 1, 1957 | Amended: 1959, 1966, 1981, 1993, 2017
Tunisia's landmark personal status code, principally drafted by President Habib Bourguiba. Abolished polygamy (criminal offense under Art. 18), replaced unilateral male repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce, set minimum marriage age. The most progressive family law in the Arab world. Covers marriage, divorce, filiation, adoption (recognition of kafala), inheritance, guardianship, and wills. Never suspended or repealed despite political pressures (2011–2013). The 2022 Constitution has not altered its operation.
Key provisions

Social and Solidarity Economy Law — Law No. 2020-30 of June 30, 2020

Adopted by ARP: June 17, 2020 | Published: JORT No. 63, July 3, 2020 (p. 1399) | Implementing decrees: still substantially outstanding as of the most recent reporting found
Tunisia's first framework statute formally recognizing "social and solidarity economy" (économie sociale et solidaire, ESS) as a distinct third sector alongside the public and private sectors. Art. 2 sets its objectives: balancing economic growth with social equity, ensuring fair coexistence of public, private, and third-sector actors, formalizing informal-economy activity, and improving the economic and social inclusion of disadvantaged and marginalized populations (isolated rural areas, unemployed youth) through cooperatives, mutual societies, and development groups. The law rests on four principles: priority of people and social purpose over capital (within sustainable-development limits), democratic one-person-one-vote governance, voluntary inter-enterprise cooperation, and statutorily limited profit distribution. It brings nine categories of entities within the ESS umbrella: cooperatives (including agricultural mutual societies), agricultural/maritime development groups, mutual associations, microfinance associations, mutual insurance companies, economically-active associations, commercial companies (excluding sole proprietorships), economic interest groupings, and future entities the legislature designates. It creates a three-tier institutional architecture — a Higher Council of the ESS (Conseil supérieur de l'ESS) at the level of the Presidency of Government, a Tunisian Instance of the ESS (Instance tunisienne de l'ESS) under the sector ministry to run a public national database/registry of ESS enterprises, and an Observatory ("Dar-ESS") to produce studies, surveys, and a satellite national account for the sector. Implementation has lagged badly: Tunisian legal commentary (Leaders.com.tn, Tunelyz) documented the core institutions as still lacking their implementing decrees more than two years after enactment, into 2023, drawing criticism for the law's heavy reliance on regulatory texts that were never issued. The related but legally distinct Community Companies framework (Decree-Law No. 2022-15, later restructured by Decree-Law No. 2025-3) has since become the more operational vehicle for collective/regional economic activity, while the ESS law's own institutions remain largely dormant on the record reviewed.

Creates "community companies" — a distinct legal form from cooperatives, framed by the presidency as a "third pillar" of the economy alongside the private and public sectors. Law No. 2025-9 of May 21, 2025Regulation of Employment Contracts and Prohibition of Labor Subcontracting

Enacted: May 21, 2025 | Published: JORT No. 61, May 23, 2025 | In force: May 23, 2025
A major reform of the Labor Code's Art. 6 contract-type regime and a flat prohibition of labor-supply subcontracting ("sous-traitance de main-d'œuvre" — one company supplying workers to another, including for guarding and cleaning services), framed as an anti-precarity measure. Key provisions
  • Open-ended contracts (CDI) become the presumed default form of employment relationship
  • Fixed-term contracts (CDD) restricted to enumerated exceptional cases: seasonal work, temporary replacement of an absent employee, or a temporary surge in activity
  • Trial (probation) periods capped at six months, renewable once only; no further probationary period may follow

Human Rights Watch, the ICJ, and EuroMed Rights condemned the move as a serious setback for human-rights accountability, noting it removes an external avenue for the very rights defenders and opposition figures documented in the Judicial Organization section's political-prosecution cases.
