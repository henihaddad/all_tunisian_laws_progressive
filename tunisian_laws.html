Tunisia's legal system is predominantly civil law-based, derived from the French legal tradition with elements of Islamic law (particularly in personal status). The unified secular judiciary was established in 1956 upon abolition of Shari'a courts. The current constitution is the 2022 Constitution (adopted July 25, 2022 by referendum; published August 18, 2022), which supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions. Official legal texts are published in the Journal Officiel de la République Tunisienne (JORT).

⚖️ Constitutional Law

Constitution of 2022Constitution of the Republic of Tunisia

Adopted: July 25, 2022 (referendum) | Published: August 18, 2022 | In force: August 2022
The current governing constitution, promulgated by Presidential Decree No. 2022-691 of August 17, 2022. Establishes a presidential system with a bicameral legislature (National Council of Regions and Districts + Assembly of People's Representatives). Supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions as well as Decree-Law No. 2011-14 of March 23, 2011. Article 5 references Islam and "the aims of Pure Islam." Comprises 142 articles organized into 10 chapters.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam is its religion, Arabic its language, the republic its form of government
  • Art. 2: Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship, the will of the people, and the supremacy of law
  • Art. 5: The state shall work to achieve the objectives of Pure Islam
  • Art. 29: Intellectual property is guaranteed
  • Art. 40: Freedom to form political parties, unions, and associations is guaranteed
  • Art. 42: Freedom of peaceful assembly and demonstration is guaranteed
  • Art. 86–90: President elected for 5-year term, limit of two terms
  • Repeals: 1959 Constitution, Decree-Law No. 2011-14, 2014 Constitution
Constitutional

Constitution of 2014 [Superseded]Constitution of the Republic of Tunisia

Adopted: January 26, 2014 | In force: February 2014 | Superseded: August 2022
Post-Arab Spring constitution adopted by the National Constituent Assembly with 200 votes for, 12 against, 4 abstentions. Comprised 149 articles across 10 chapters. Established a semi-presidential system. Article 74 restricted the presidency to Muslims. Now superseded by the 2022 Constitution.
Key provisions (historical)
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam its religion, Arabic its language
  • Art. 2: Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship, the will of the people, and the primacy of law
  • Art. 20: Equality before the law guaranteed without discrimination
  • Art. 46: Enshrined women's rights and gender parity in elected bodies
  • Art. 74: Presidential candidates must be Muslim, Tunisian nationals, born of Tunisian parents
  • Art. 88: Mechanism for presidential impeachment
  • Art. 115: Prime minister removal required majority of assembly members

Constitution of 1959 [Superseded]Constitution of the Tunisian Republic

Promulgated: June 1, 1959 | In force: June 1, 1959 | Superseded: August 2022
Tunisia's first post-independence constitution. Modeled on the French system, vested strong executive power in a president. Arabic declared national language. Presidents elected for 5-year terms. Amended multiple times — notably in 2002 (removed presidential term limits, opened candidacy to persons over 75). In force for 63 years until replaced by the 2022 Constitution.
Key provisions (historical)
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam its religion, Arabic its language, the republic its form
  • Art. 39: Presidential term set at 5 years
  • Art. 40: President elected by universal direct suffrage
  • 2002 amendment: removed two-term limit, raised age ceiling to 75

Enacted: March 23, 2011 | In force: March 2011 | Superseded: August 2022
Established the transitional constitutional framework following the 2011 revolution. Organized interim executive and legislative powers pending the drafting and adoption of a permanent constitution. Superseded by the 2022 Constitution.

📜 Civil Law & Law of Obligations

Code of Obligations and Contracts (COC) — 1906Majallat Al-Iltizamat wa-l-Uqud

Promulgated: December 15, 1906 (under French Protectorate) | In force: January 1, 1907
The foundational civil code governing contracts, torts, and obligations. Derived from the French civil law tradition, adapted for Tunisian context. Covers general theory of obligations, specific contracts (sale, lease, agency, loan, deposit, surety), and civil liability. Has been substantially amended multiple times since independence. Remains in force as the core private law instrument.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–106: General theory of obligations (formation, validity, effects, extinction)
  • Art. 13: Requirements for valid contract — capacity, consent, object, cause
  • Art. 62–80: Nullity of contracts (absolute vs. relative)
  • Art. 82–96: Effects of obligations; performance and non-performance
  • Art. 100: Damages for non-performance; principle of full reparation
  • Art. 222–262: Contract of sale (conditions, effects, warranty)
  • Art. 728–733: Civil liability for delict and quasi-delict
  • Art. 786–828: Mandate and agency

Code of Civil and Commercial Procedure — 1959Majallat Al-Murafa'at Al-Madaniyya wa-l-Tijariyya

Enacted: 1959 | Multiple amendments
Governs civil and commercial litigation procedures including jurisdiction, service of process, evidence, trials, judgments, and appeals. Regulates enforcement of judgments and provisional measures. Organizes appeals to Courts of First Instance, Courts of Appeal, and the Court of Cassation.
Key provisions
  • Rules on jurisdiction: territorial and subject-matter
  • Provisional and conservatory measures
  • Rules of evidence and burden of proof
  • Default judgments and opposition procedures
  • Appeals and cassation procedures
  • Enforcement of domestic and foreign judgments

Establishes the civil registration system for Tunisian nationals including registration of births, deaths, marriages, divorces, and changes of nationality. Mandates registration within specified time limits. Organizes the function of civil status officers (officiers d'état civil).

Nationality Code — Law No. 63-6 of February 28, 1963

Enacted: February 28, 1963 | In force: 1963 | Amended: 1975, 2002, 2010
Defines Tunisian nationality by birth, descent, naturalization, and reacquisition. Originally limited transmission of nationality through the paternal line; amended in 2010 to allow Tunisian mothers married to foreign nationals to transmit nationality to their children. Governs loss, renunciation, and deprivation of nationality.
Key provisions
  • Art. 6: Nationality acquired by birth to a Tunisian father (jus sanguinis)
  • Art. 12: Nationality acquired through naturalization (5-year residence requirement)
  • 2010 amendment: children of Tunisian mother and foreign father may acquire nationality
  • Art. 30–35: Loss and deprivation of Tunisian nationality

👪 Personal Status Law

The Code of Personal Status (CPS) is Tunisia's most internationally distinctive statute — it abolishes polygamy and replaced unilateral repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce. It has been continuously in force since 1957.

Code of Personal Status (CPS) — Beylical Decree of August 13, 1956Majallat Al-Ahwal Al-Shakhsiyya

Enacted: August 13, 1956 (beylical decree under Lamine Bey) | In force: January 1, 1957 | Amended: 1959, 1966, 1981, 1993, 2017
Tunisia's landmark personal status code, principally drafted by President Habib Bourguiba. Abolished polygamy (criminal offense under Art. 18), replaced unilateral male repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce, set minimum marriage age. The most progressive family law in the Arab world. Covers marriage, divorce, filiation, adoption (recognition of kafala), inheritance, guardianship, and wills. Never suspended or repealed despite political pressures (2011–2013). The 2022 Constitution has not altered its operation.
Key provisions
  • Art. 3: Marriage requires consent of both spouses; minimum age 18 (with exceptions)
  • Art. 5: Marriage must be registered and conducted before a civil status officer
  • Art. 18: Polygamy prohibited and criminalized — up to 1 year imprisonment + fine for marrying while already married
  • Art. 29–37: Divorce only by court order; grounds include mutual consent, harm, abandonment
  • Art. 31: Either spouse may initiate divorce; court-ordered alimony mandatory
  • Art. 46–67: Filiation and parentage; paternity presumption
  • Art. 68–78: Legal guardianship (tutelle) of minors
  • Art. 85–199: Inheritance rules (miras) — blends civil and Islamic principles
  • Art. 154–165: Guardianship over persons under disability
  • 1993 amendment: mother gains equal custody rights; divorced mothers may become guardians
  • 2017 amendment: allowed Muslim women to marry non-Muslim men (circular, not codified)

Child Protection Code — Law No. 95-92 of November 9, 1995Majallat Himayat Al-Tifl

Enacted: November 9, 1995 | In force: 1995 | Amended: 2002, 2008
Comprehensive statute protecting minors from abuse, neglect, exploitation, and trafficking. Establishes the Children's Judge (juge des enfants) with jurisdiction over child protection cases. Covers child welfare, delinquency, protective placement, and guardianship. Implements Tunisia's obligations under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (ratified 1992).
Key provisions
  • Creates specialized Children's Judges and children's courts
  • Art. 24–30: Protective measures for children in danger
  • Art. 70–80: Juvenile delinquency proceedings; preference for rehabilitation over punishment
  • Art. 101: Prohibition of child labor below age 16 in principle
  • Art. 105–110: International child abduction; return mechanisms
Personal StatusCivil

Law No. 58-27 of March 4, 1958Kafala (Legal Sponsorship of Abandoned Children)

Enacted: March 4, 1958 | In force: 1958 | Amended: 1994
Regulates the kafala system — the legal guardianship of abandoned children (Tunisia does not recognize formal adoption in the Islamic sense). Allows Tunisian families to take in abandoned children as legal dependents. Creates rights and obligations equivalent to adoption without severing the child's original lineage.
Personal Status

Law No. 2017-58 of August 11, 2017Elimination of Violence Against Women

Enacted: August 11, 2017 | In force: February 2018
Landmark statute criminalizing all forms of violence against women — physical, psychological, sexual, and economic. Abolished the provision allowing rapists to escape prosecution by marrying their victim. Created specialized police units and prosecution procedures for gender-based violence. Established protective orders and victim shelters. Considered one of the most progressive anti-GBV laws in the Arab world.
Key provisions
  • Removed Art. 227 bis of the Penal Code (marriage escaping rape prosecution)
  • Defines and criminalizes economic, psychological, and sexual violence
  • Establishes specialized prosecution units for violence against women
  • Provides emergency protective orders for victims
  • Mandatory reporting obligations for health professionals
  • National Observatory on Violence Against Women created

⚔️ Criminal Law & Procedure

Penal Code — Law of July 9, 1913 (as amended)Majallat Al-Qanun Al-Jaza'i

Originally enacted: July 9, 1913 (French Protectorate) | Revised: 1964, 1968, 1987, 1993, 1999, 2004, 2010, 2017
Tunisia's criminal code. Enacted under the French Protectorate in 1913, substantially revised after independence. Classifies offenses into crimes (felonies), délits (misdemeanors), and contraventions (infractions). Organized in two books: general provisions and specific offenses. Notable provisions include criminalization of same-sex relations (Art. 230), blasphemy provisions, and offenses against state security. Amended significantly in 2017 following the Violence Against Women Law.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–59: General principles — criminal liability, complicity, recidivism, penalties
  • Art. 52: Capital punishment available for certain crimes (murder, rape causing death, terrorism)
  • Art. 72–85: Offenses against state security (treason, espionage)
  • Art. 86: Attacks against the head of state or government
  • Art. 121: Prohibited associations
  • Art. 214: Abortion prohibited except to save mother's life
  • Art. 218: Homicide and serious assault
  • Art. 226: Offenses against public morals
  • Art. 226 bis: Defamation of public officials via new communication technologies
  • Art. 227: Rape — minimum 6 years imprisonment
  • Art. 230: Consensual same-sex relations criminalized — up to 3 years imprisonment
  • Art. 231–245: Theft, fraud, embezzlement
  • Art. 305: Receiving stolen goods

Code of Criminal Procedure — Law No. 68-23 of July 24, 1968

Enacted: July 24, 1968 | Published: JORT No. 32 (August 2–6, 1968) | Amended: Law No. 87-70 (Nov. 26, 1987), Law No. 93-114 (Nov. 22, 1993)
Governs all stages of criminal proceedings in Tunisia. Organized in five books covering: public action and preliminary investigation, trial court jurisdictions, extraordinary remedies (appeals, cassation), special procedures, and execution of sentences. Article 1 establishes that any infraction gives rise to public action aimed at applying penalties.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1: Any infraction triggers public action ("Toute infraction donne ouverture à une action publique")
  • Art. 13–14 (as amended by Law No. 87-70): Limits on garde à vue detention
  • Art. 47: Preliminary judicial investigation mandatory for crimes (felonies)
  • Art. 57: Rights of suspects during investigation
  • Art. 85–87 (as amended by Law No. 93-114): Preventive detention — initial 6-month maximum, extendable
  • Art. 106–107: Extended detention provisions
  • Book II: Jurisdiction of cantonal courts, courts of first instance, courts of appeal
  • Book III: Opposition, appeal, and cassation remedies
  • Book IV: Military courts, terrorism courts, special jurisdictions
  • Book V: Execution of criminal sentences

Anti-Terrorism Law — Law No. 2015-26 of July 7, 2015

Enacted: July 7, 2015 | In force: July 2015
Comprehensive counter-terrorism statute enacted following the Bardo Museum and Sousse beach attacks (2015). Replaced the 2003 anti-terrorism law. Defines terrorism broadly, criminalizes financing, propaganda, recruitment, and travel for terrorist purposes. Establishes specialized anti-terrorism courts and a national anti-terrorism agency (CTAF). Criticized by human rights groups for overly broad definitions.
Key provisions
  • Broad definition of "terrorist act" including threats to public order
  • Criminalization of glorification of terrorism and incitement
  • Criminalizes financing of terrorism and related financial flows
  • Prohibition on travel to conflict zones for terrorist purposes
  • Specialized Terrorism Chamber at the Court of First Instance of Tunis
  • Extended detention periods for terrorism suspects
  • Asset freezing without prior judicial authorization
CriminalSecurity

Enacted: March 7, 2017 | In force: 2017
Requires elected officials, senior public servants, and government appointees to declare assets, income, and interests. Creates the National Anti-Corruption Authority (INLUCC — Instance Nationale de Lutte contre la Corruption). Whistleblower protections included.

Law No. 2015-26 / AML — Law No. 2019-23 of March 6, 2019Anti-Money Laundering and Financing of Terrorism

Enacted: March 6, 2019 | Replaced: Law No. 2003-75
Brings Tunisia into compliance with FATF recommendations. Criminalizes money laundering and terrorist financing. Imposes KYC (know-your-customer) obligations on financial institutions, notaries, accountants, and real estate agents. Establishes the Financial Intelligence Unit (CTAF — Commission Tunisienne des Analyses Financières).

Law No. 92-52 of May 18, 1992Narcotics and Psychotropic Substances

Enacted: May 18, 1992 | In force: 1992 | Amended: 1999, 2017
Governs narcotics and psychotropic substances including cultivation, production, trafficking, possession, and use. Imposes severe penalties — mandatory minimum 1 year for consumption (Art. 4), up to life imprisonment for trafficking. Article 4 on personal drug use attracted international criticism; the 2017 amendment introduced some discretion for judges in consumption cases.
Key provisions
  • Art. 4: Personal consumption of narcotics — 1 to 5 years imprisonment + fine (mandatory)
  • Art. 5: Trafficking — 6 years to life imprisonment
  • Art. 12: Confiscation of proceeds and instruments of offenses
  • 2017 amendment: judges given discretion on sentencing for first-time consumption offenses
Criminal

Decree No. 2022-54 of September 13, 2022Combating Information Technology Offenses

Enacted: September 13, 2022 | In force: September 2022
Criminalizes "false information" spread online that "harms third parties, public order, or national defense." Criticized by press freedom organizations for its vague language and potential to criminalize journalism and free speech. Passed by presidential decree without parliamentary scrutiny.

🏪 Commercial Law

Commercial Code — Law No. 59-129 of October 5, 1959Majallat Al-Tijara

Enacted: October 5, 1959 | In force: January 1, 1960 | Amended: multiple times (through 2007+)
Core statute governing commercial activities, traders, and commercial acts in Tunisia. Defines the status of traders (commerçants), regulates commercial registers, commercial agencies, and trade names. Covers bills of exchange, promissory notes, cheques, and commercial contracts. Has been substantially amended since 1960 to modernize commercial law.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–15: Definition of commercial acts and traders
  • Art. 16–30: Commercial register obligations
  • Art. 57–90: Agency and commercial representation
  • Art. 192–260: Bills of exchange (lettres de change)
  • Art. 261–290: Promissory notes (billets à ordre)
  • Art. 291–325: Cheques — form, endorsement, payment, protest
  • Art. 400+: Commercial leases (fonds de commerce)

Companies Code — Law No. 2000-93 of November 3, 2000Majallat Al-Sharikaat

Enacted: November 3, 2000 | In force: 2001 | Amended: 2002, 2005, 2009, 2016, 2019
Comprehensive statute regulating all forms of companies in Tunisia. Governs formation, management, operation, auditing, transformation, merger, and dissolution of companies. Covers: SARL (private limited company), SA (joint-stock company), SNC (general partnership), SCS (limited partnership), and cooperative societies.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–50: General provisions applicable to all companies
  • Art. 51–128: Joint-stock companies (Sociétés Anonymes — SA): board of directors, general meetings, auditors
  • Art. 129–165: Private limited companies (SARL): capital minimum, management
  • Art. 166–200: General partnerships (SNC)
  • Art. 201–230: Limited partnerships (SCS, SCA)
  • Mandatory: external statutory auditor (commissaire aux comptes) for SA
  • Minority shareholder protection provisions
  • 2019 amendment: introduced single-member SARL (SUARL)

Collective Proceedings Code — Law No. 1995-34 of April 17, 1995Insolvency and Business Recovery

Enacted: April 17, 1995 | In force: 1995 | Amended: 1999, 2003, 2016
Governs insolvency, bankruptcy, judicial reorganization (redressement judiciaire), and liquidation of insolvent businesses. Establishes procedures for appointing judicial administrators and liquidators, protecting creditors, and rehabilitating viable enterprises. Amended in 2016 to expand preventive settlement mechanisms.
Key provisions
  • Preventive settlement (règlement amiable) for pre-insolvency situations
  • Judicial recovery (redressement judiciaire): 6-month observation period
  • Liquidation (faillite): asset realization and creditor distribution
  • Ranking of creditors: secured, preferred, unsecured
  • Art. 36: Suspension of creditor actions during reorganization

👷 Labor Law

Labor Code — Law No. 66-27 of April 30, 1966Majallat Al-Shughl

Enacted: April 30, 1966 | In force: May 1966 | Amended: 1994, 1996, 2006, 2007, 2011, 2017
The primary statute governing employment relationships in Tunisia. Covers individual employment contracts, conditions of work, wages, termination, workplace safety, collective bargaining, and trade unions. Applies to the private sector; public servants are governed by the General Statute of Civil Servants. Implements ILO core labor conventions (Tunisia has ratified 62 ILO conventions).
Key provisions
  • Art. 6: Employment contract — types (fixed-term, indefinite-term, project-based)
  • Art. 14–22: Trial period (période d'essai)
  • Art. 23–35: Obligations of employer and employee
  • Art. 40: Minimum wage (SMIG/SMAG) — set by decree
  • Art. 73–83: Annual paid leave — minimum 1 day per month (18 days/year)
  • Art. 90–100: Work hours — 48 hours/week maximum (40 hours in industry)
  • Art. 120: Dismissal — requires just cause; procedural requirements for economic dismissal
  • Art. 123: Severance pay for unfair dismissal
  • Art. 152: Trade union rights; freedom of association
  • Art. 155: Right to strike (with pre-notice requirements)
  • Art. 193–200: Labor inspectorate powers
  • Art. 230–240: Occupational health and safety requirements
LaborAmended

Enacted: December 12, 1983 | In force: 1984 | Amended: 2007, 2011, 2017
Governs the employment conditions, rights, and obligations of Tunisian civil servants (fonctionnaires). Covers recruitment, grades, promotions, disciplinary procedures, leave, retirement, and termination. Complemented by specific statutes for particular sectors (teachers, judges, military, etc.).

Enacted: December 14, 1960 | In force: 1961 | Amended: extensively
Establishes the compulsory social security system. Organizes the National Social Security Fund (CNSS for private sector) and the National Retirement and Social Welfare Fund (CNRPS for public sector). Covers retirement pensions, invalidity, survivors' benefits, occupational accidents, and maternity allowances.

🏛️ Administrative Law

Administrative Court Law — Law No. 72-40 of June 1, 1972

Enacted: June 1, 1972 | In force: 1972 | Amended: multiple times
Establishes the Administrative Court (Tribunal Administratif) as the supreme administrative jurisdiction. Has jurisdiction over disputes between citizens and public bodies, annulment of illegal administrative acts, and administrative contracts. Also serves as the Supreme Audit Institution reviewing public accounts. Organized into chambers; appeals go to Administrative Court plenary.
Administrative

Local Government Code — Law No. 2018-29 of May 9, 2018Majallat Al-Jama'aat Al-Mahaliyya

Enacted: May 9, 2018 | In force: May 2018 | Implements: 2014 Constitution Chapter VII
Comprehensive decentralization legislation replacing the previous municipalities law. Organizes three levels of local government: municipalities (balladiyyat), regions (wilayat), and districts (aqalim). Establishes directly-elected municipal councils with legislative powers in local matters. Introduces participatory budgeting and citizens' right to petition. Implemented after 2018 municipal elections.
Key provisions
  • Three levels: municipality, region (governorate), district
  • Directly elected councils at all three levels
  • Fiscal autonomy — own revenue sources and transfers from central budget
  • Participatory democracy mechanisms (citizen petitions, public consultation)
  • Gender parity requirement for municipal council lists
  • Youth quota (minimum 1/3 of each list under 35)
Administrative

Public Procurement Decree — Decree No. 2014-1039 of March 13, 2014

Enacted: March 13, 2014 | In force: 2014 | Amended: 2022
Governs all public procurement by state entities, public establishments, and local authorities. Establishes competitive bidding requirements, transparency obligations, evaluation criteria, and remedies for tenderers. Creates the National Commission for Public Procurement Investigations (HAICOP). Sets thresholds for direct award, restricted tendering, and open tendering.
Administrative

Access to Information Decree — Decree-Law No. 2011-41 of May 26, 2011

Enacted: May 26, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Strengthened by 2016 Organic Law
Grants citizens the right to access information held by public bodies. Establishes a 20-day response deadline for requests. Replaced in part by the more comprehensive Organic Law No. 2016-22 of March 24, 2016, which created an independent Access to Information Commission (IVD predecessor).
AdministrativePress

Enacted: July 11, 2016 | In force: 2017
Authorizes and regulates e-government services. Allows administrative procedures to be conducted electronically. Establishes standards for electronic authentication, digital archives, and online payment to the state. Part of Tunisia's e-government reform strategy.

💰 Tax & Fiscal Law

Income Tax Code — Law No. 89-114 of December 30, 1989Code de l'Impôt sur le Revenu des Personnes Physiques et l'Impôt sur les Sociétés

Enacted: December 30, 1989 | In force: January 1, 1990 | Amended: annually in Finance Laws
Governs personal income tax (IRPP) and corporate income tax (IS) in Tunisia. Personal income tax is progressive with rates from 0% to 35%. Corporate income tax standard rate is 15%, with higher rates for banks and telecoms (35%) and reduced rates for SMEs and certain sectors. Amended annually through the Finance Law.
Key rates (as of 2024)
  • Personal income tax: 0% up to 5,000 TND, 26% bracket, 35% top rate
  • Corporate income tax: 15% standard rate
  • Banks, financial institutions, telecoms: 35%
  • SMEs: reduced rate of 10% (conditions apply)
  • Withholding tax on dividends: 10%
  • Capital gains tax: included in income tax
TaxAmended

VAT Code — Law No. 88-61 of June 2, 1988Code de la Taxe sur la Valeur Ajoutée

Enacted: June 2, 1988 | In force: January 1, 1989 | Amended: annually
Establishes value-added tax in Tunisia. Standard rate 19%; reduced rates of 13% (certain services) and 7% (basic goods, agricultural products). Exports are zero-rated. Registration required for businesses with turnover exceeding 100,000 TND/year (services) or 200,000 TND (goods).
TaxAmended

Tax Procedures Code — Law No. 2000-82 of August 9, 2000Code des Droits et Procédures Fiscaux

Enacted: August 9, 2000 | In force: 2001 | Amended: annually
Unifies and governs all tax administration procedures including filing obligations, tax audits, assessment, payment, penalties, and dispute resolution. Establishes taxpayers' rights during tax audits. Provides for administrative appeals before tax committees and judicial appeals before administrative courts.
Tax

Customs Code — Law No. 2008-77 of December 22, 2008

Enacted: December 22, 2008 | In force: 2009 | Replaced: 1955 Customs Code
Governs importation, exportation, transit, and customs duties. Establishes the legal framework for customs declarations, inspections, duty rates, and customs offenses. Aligns Tunisia's customs procedures with international standards (WCO Revised Kyoto Convention). Governs free zones and customs warehouses.

Regulates taxes and fees collected by municipalities including property taxes (TFP — taxe foncière sur les propriétés bâties et non bâties), tourism taxes, advertising taxes, and local business taxes (TCL — taxe sur les établissements à caractère industriel, commercial ou professionnel).

🏦 Financial & Banking Law

Banking Law — Law No. 2016-48 of July 11, 2016

Enacted: July 11, 2016 | In force: 2016 | Replaced: Law No. 2001-65
Regulates credit institutions, banking activities, and financial services in Tunisia. Establishes licensing requirements for banks and microfinance institutions. Strengthens the supervisory powers of the Central Bank of Tunisia (BCT). Introduces new categories of banking licenses and regulates Islamic banking activities.
Key provisions
  • Licensing: exclusive reserve of banking activities for licensed credit institutions
  • Capital requirements: minimum TND 25 million for universal banks
  • Supervision: BCT has powers to inspect, sanction, and revoke licenses
  • Islamic banking: participatory banks regulated under specific provisions
  • Deposit guarantee: Fund for Deposit Guarantee created
  • Resolution: framework for failing banks established
Financial

Central Bank Law — Law No. 2016-35 of April 25, 2016

Enacted: April 25, 2016 | In force: 2016 | Replaced: Law No. 58-90 (1958)
Establishes the Banque Centrale de Tunisie (BCT) as an independent institution responsible for monetary policy, exchange rate management, payment systems oversight, and banking supervision. Grants operational independence to the BCT Governor. Prohibits direct central bank financing of the state budget deficit.
Financial

Insurance Code — Law No. 92-24 of February 9, 1992

Enacted: February 9, 1992 | In force: 1992 | Amended: 1997, 2002, 2008, 2014
Governs all insurance and reinsurance activities in Tunisia. Mandatory insurance categories include third-party motor liability, employer liability, and professional liability for certain professions. Regulates insurance contract terms, claims procedures, and policyholder protections. Insurance companies supervised by the Insurance General Committee (CGA).
Financial

Microfinance Law — Law No. 2011-117 of November 5, 2011

Enacted: November 5, 2011 | In force: 2011
Liberalizes and regulates the microfinance sector, allowing private and associative organizations to offer microcredit services beyond the former state monopoly (Banque Tunisienne de Solidarité). Establishes the Supervisory Authority for Microfinance (ACM). Maximum loan amounts and interest rate caps specified.
FinancialSocial

Financial Market Law — Law No. 94-117 of November 14, 1994

Enacted: November 14, 1994 | In force: 1995 | Amended: 1999, 2005, 2016
Governs the Tunisian stock exchange (Bourse des Valeurs Mobilières de Tunis — BVMT), securities regulation, and capital markets. Creates the Financial Market Council (CMF — Conseil du Marché Financier) as the securities regulator. Regulates public offers, disclosure requirements, insider trading, and market manipulation.

📈 Investment & Business Environment

Investment Law — Law No. 2016-71 of September 30, 2016

Enacted: September 30, 2016 | In force: April 2017 | Replaced: Investment Code of 1993
Tunisia's primary investment statute, replacing the 1993 Investment Code. Applies to both domestic and foreign investment. Guarantees freedom of investment, equal treatment of foreign and domestic investors, protection against expropriation (with compensation), and transfer of profits and capital. Establishes the Investment Authority (FIPA — Foreign Investment Promotion Agency) and the Investment Development Agency (IDI). Creates investment incentives including tax benefits for priority sectors and regions.
Key provisions
  • Freedom of establishment for foreign investors (with exceptions in certain sectors)
  • Art. 7: Equal treatment of foreign investors
  • Art. 21–25: Protection against nationalization and expropriation
  • Art. 26: Freedom to transfer capital, profits, and salaries of foreign staff
  • Incentive framework: preferential tax rates for investment in priority sectors
  • Regional development incentives for investments in interior regions
  • Foreign ownership: up to 100% in most sectors; limited in certain strategic sectors
  • Single window (guichet unique) for investment approvals
CommercialFinancial

Free Zones Law — Law No. 92-81 of August 3, 1992

Enacted: August 3, 1992 | In force: 1993 | Amended: 2007, 2009, 2019
Creates special economic zones (free zones) offering customs and tax advantages for export-oriented businesses. Free zone companies are exempt from import duties, VAT on local purchases, and income tax for initial periods. Currently active free zones include Bizerte Economic Activity Park. Requires minimum 70% export of production.
CommercialTax

Startup Act — Law No. 2018-20 of April 17, 2018

Enacted: April 17, 2018 | In force: August 2018
Innovative legislation creating a special legal and fiscal framework for innovative startups. Eligible startups receive a "Startup" label granting advantages including: salary coverage for founders for 1 year, exemption from employer social security contributions for 3 years, preferential tax treatment, facilitated foreign currency access, and streamlined administrative procedures. One of Africa's first dedicated startup laws.
Key provisions
  • "Startup" label granted by the Startup Tunisia Committee
  • Founder salary covered by the state for up to 1 year
  • 3-year exemption from employer social security contributions
  • 5-year corporate tax holiday
  • Foreign currency account allowed for startups
  • Fast-track work permits for foreign talent
  • Crowdfunding and convertible notes facilitated
CommercialDigital

Arbitration Code — Law No. 93-42 of April 26, 1993

Enacted: April 26, 1993 | In force: 1993
Governs domestic and international commercial arbitration in Tunisia. Based on the UNCITRAL Model Law. Provides for arbitration agreements, constitution of tribunals, conduct of proceedings, and recognition and enforcement of awards. Tunisia ratified the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards in 1967.
Commercial

💡 Intellectual Property Law

Literary and Artistic Property Law — Law No. 2009-33 of June 23, 2009

Enacted: June 23, 2009 | In force: 2009 | Replaced: Law No. 94-36
Governs copyright and related rights in Tunisia. Protects literary, artistic, and musical works, software, databases, and audiovisual productions. Protection term: author's life + 70 years. Covers moral rights (inalienable) and economic rights (transferable). Aligns with WIPO Copyright Treaty and TRIPS Agreement obligations. Enforced by the Tunisian Copyright Protection Office (OTPDA).
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–10: Scope — literary, artistic, musical, audiovisual, photographic works; software
  • Art. 15: Moral rights (right of integrity, right of attribution) — inalienable and perpetual
  • Art. 16–22: Economic rights — reproduction, distribution, public performance
  • Art. 24: Duration — life of author + 70 years
  • Art. 45–55: Related rights for performers, producers, broadcasters
  • Art. 60–75: Exceptions and limitations (private use, education, libraries)
  • Art. 87–100: Civil and criminal remedies for infringement
IP

Industrial Property Law — Law No. 2000-84 of August 24, 2000

Enacted: August 24, 2000 | In force: 2001 | Amended: 2007
Governs patents, trademarks, geographical indications, industrial designs, and utility models in Tunisia. Administered by the Tunisian Intellectual Property Office (INNORPI). Tunisia is a member of WIPO, the Paris Convention, the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), and the Madrid Protocol on trademarks.
Key provisions
  • Patents: 20-year protection from filing date; requires novelty, inventive step, industrial application
  • Trademarks: 10-year protection, renewable; registration at INNORPI required
  • Industrial designs: 5-year protection, renewable up to 4 times (25 years total)
  • Geographical indications: protection for Tunisian products with geographic origin
  • Compulsory licenses: available for public health or national emergency
IP

🌿 Environmental Law

Framework Environmental Law — Law No. 88-91 of August 2, 1988

Enacted: August 2, 1988 | In force: 1988 | Amended: 1992, 2001
Tunisia's foundational environmental statute, creating the National Environment Protection Agency (ANPE). Establishes environmental impact assessment (EIA) requirements for major projects. Governs pollution prevention, waste management, and natural resource protection. Establishes the principle of "polluter pays."
Environmental

Water Code — Law No. 75-16 of March 31, 1975

Enacted: March 31, 1975 | In force: 1975 | Amended: 1987, 2001
Classifies all water resources (surface and groundwater) as public domain. Governs water use permits, irrigation rights, water conservation, and protection from pollution. Establishes the National Water Authority and regional water committees.
Environmental

Forestry Code — Law No. 88-20 of March 13, 1988

Enacted: March 13, 1988 | In force: 1988
Classifies and protects Tunisian forests and natural parks. Regulates logging permits, forest fires (criminalized), afforestation, and pastoral use of forest land. Creates the Directorate General of Forests under the Ministry of Agriculture.
Environmental

Waste Management Law — Law No. 96-41 of June 10, 1996

Enacted: June 10, 1996 | In force: 1996 | Amended: 2007
Governs waste prevention, collection, transport, treatment, and disposal. Creates the National Agency for Waste Management (ANGed). Establishes the waste hierarchy (prevention, reuse, recycling, recovery, disposal). Special provisions for hazardous waste, medical waste, and industrial effluents.
Environmental

Law No. 2015-12 of May 11, 2015Energy Transition and Green Economy

Enacted: May 11, 2015 | In force: 2015
Framework law for energy transition and promotion of renewable energy. Sets national targets for renewable energy share (30% by 2030, updated to 35%). Regulates self-generation of electricity from renewables, feed-in tariffs, and investment in the energy sector. Establishes the National Agency for Energy Conservation (ANME).
Environmental

📰 Press, Media & Freedom of Expression

Press Decree — Decree-Law No. 2011-115 of November 2, 2011

Enacted: November 2, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Replaced: Press Code of 1975
Post-revolution statute guaranteeing freedom of the press and abolishing prior government control over media. Abolished the requirement for prior authorization to publish newspapers. Criminalized defamation but with strong protections for press freedom. Created the High Authority for Audio-Visual Communication (HAICA — Haute Autorité Indépendante de la Communication Audiovisuelle).
Press

HAICA Organic Law — Decree-Law No. 2011-116 of November 2, 2011

Enacted: November 2, 2011 | In force: 2011
Establishes the High Authority for Audio-Visual Communication (HAICA) as an independent regulatory body for radio and television broadcasting. HAICA grants broadcasting licenses, sets content standards, and monitors compliance. Replaced the previous state-controlled broadcasting authority.
Press

Access to Information Organic Law — Law No. 2016-22 of March 24, 2016

Enacted: March 24, 2016 | In force: 2017
Comprehensive right-to-information law creating an independent Access to Information Authority (IVD — Instance d'Accès à l'Information). Any person may request information from public bodies; response within 20 days required. Narrow exceptions for national security, privacy, and commercial confidentiality. Considered one of the strongest RTI laws in the MENA region.
PressAdministrative

💻 Digital Law & ICT

Personal Data Protection Law — Law No. 2004-63 of July 27, 2004

Enacted: July 27, 2004 | In force: 2004 | Amended: 2013
Tunisia's data protection statute — one of the first in Africa. Creates the National Authority for Personal Data Protection (INPDP — Instance Nationale de Protection des Données Personnelles). Requires prior authorization from INPDP before processing sensitive personal data. Establishes data subject rights (access, rectification, deletion) and obligations on data controllers.
Key provisions
  • Mandatory registration of personal data processing with INPDP
  • Prior authorization for sensitive data (health, biometric, political, religious)
  • Data subject rights: access, rectification, objection, deletion
  • Restrictions on cross-border data transfers
  • Security obligations for data controllers
  • Criminal sanctions for violations: up to 2 years + fines
Digital

Cybercrime Law — Law No. 99-89 of August 2, 1999(as amended)

Enacted: August 2, 1999 | In force: 1999 | Amended: 2004, 2010
Criminalizes unauthorized access to computer systems, interception of electronic communications, introduction of malicious code, and computer fraud. Establishes penalties of 2 to 5 years imprisonment for basic offenses; up to 8 years for attacks on critical infrastructure. Pre-dates the current Digital Code effort.
DigitalCriminal

Telecommunications Code — Law No. 2001-1 of January 15, 2001

Enacted: January 15, 2001 | In force: 2001 | Amended: 2002, 2008, 2013
Liberalizes the telecommunications sector and establishes the independent regulator (INT — Instance Nationale des Télécommunications). Governs licensing of operators, frequency management, interconnection, universal service obligations, and quality standards. Opened mobile and internet markets to competition.

Public Health Code — Law No. 91-63 of July 29, 1991

Enacted: July 29, 1991 | In force: 1992 | Amended: 1994, 2002, 2008, 2019
Framework law for public health in Tunisia. Covers organization of the health system, health facilities (public and private), medical professions, pharmaceutical activities, and preventive health. Establishes obligations for health establishments and practitioners. Governs mandatory vaccination programs and epidemic disease control.
Health

Pharmacy Law — Law No. 85-91 of September 11, 1985

Enacted: September 11, 1985 | In force: 1986 | Amended: 1992, 2004
Governs pharmaceutical activities including manufacturing, import, distribution, and dispensing of medicines. Requires prior authorization for drug marketing (visa d'exploitation). Regulates pharmacy ownership (restricted to licensed pharmacists). Establishes the National Drug Authority (Agence Nationale de Contrôle Sanitaire et Environnemental des Produits — ANCSEP).
Health

Mental Health Law — Law No. 92-83 of August 3, 1992

Enacted: August 3, 1992 | In force: 1992
Governs mental health care and protection of psychiatric patients' rights. Regulates voluntary and involuntary hospitalization, patient consent, and judicial oversight of psychiatric confinement. Establishes conditions for involuntary placement and patients' right to legal representation.
Health

🎓 Education Law

Education Framework Law — Law No. 2002-80 of July 23, 2002

Enacted: July 23, 2002 | In force: 2002 | Replaced: Law No. 91-65
Framework law organizing the Tunisian national education system. Establishes compulsory schooling for 6–16 year olds. Organizes the three-stage system: basic education (9 years), secondary education (4 years), and higher education. Defines national curriculum objectives, teacher qualifications, and school governance.
Education

Higher Education Law — Law No. 2008-19 of February 25, 2008

Enacted: February 25, 2008 | In force: 2008
Governs universities, higher institutes, and research establishments. Introduces the LMD (Licence–Master–Doctorat) system aligned with the Bologna Process. Regulates accreditation, quality assurance, student rights, and university governance. Establishes the framework for private higher education institutions.

🤝 Social Security & Protection

CNSS Organic Law — Law No. 60-30 of December 14, 1960 (as amended)

Enacted: 1960 | Extensively amended through 2024
Establishes the National Social Security Fund (Caisse Nationale de Sécurité Sociale — CNSS) for private sector workers. Covers contributory retirement pensions, invalidity, death benefits, occupational hazards, family allowances, and maternity. Employee contribution: 9.18%; Employer contribution: 16.57% of gross wages.
SocialLabor

Social Assistance Program Law — Law No. 2019-49 of June 6, 2019AMEN Social

Enacted: June 6, 2019 | In force: 2020
Creates Tunisia's unified social protection system "AMEN Social" replacing fragmented assistance programs. Provides means-tested direct cash transfers to poor families (National Aid Program — PAN), health coverage for the poor (AMGP), and subsidized access to services. Governed by the Ministry of Social Affairs.
Social

Disability Law — Law No. 2005-83 of August 15, 2005Promotion and Protection of Persons with Disabilities

Enacted: August 15, 2005 | In force: 2005
Framework law for the rights and inclusion of persons with disabilities. Prohibits discrimination in employment, education, and public services. Requires accessibility in public buildings and transportation. Establishes disability quota of 1% in public sector employment. Creates the National Bureau of Disabled Persons.
Social

🏠 Land & Property Law

Real Property Registration Law — Law of July 1, 1885 (as reformed)Immatriculation Foncière

Originally: 1885 (protectorate) | Overhauled: Law No. 65-5 of February 12, 1965 | Amended: 1992, 2005
Governs land title registration (immatriculation foncière) through the Property Rights Registry (Conservation de la Propriété Foncière). Once registered, title is conclusive and indefeasible. Unregistered land may be subject to adverse possession claims. Tunisia operates a Torrens-style title system for registered properties.

Urban Planning Code — Law No. 94-122 of November 28, 1994Code de l'Aménagement du Territoire et de l'Urbanisme

Enacted: November 28, 1994 | In force: 1995 | Amended: 2003, 2009, 2016
Governs land use planning, building permits, zoning, and urban development in Tunisia. Requires detailed urban plans (PAD — Plans d'Aménagement de Détail) for urban areas. Establishes building permit requirements, setback rules, and height restrictions. Regulates real estate development and subdivision approvals.
LandAdministrative

Housing Law — Law No. 77-46 of May 30, 1977Rental Relations and Tenant Protections

Enacted: May 30, 1977 | In force: 1977 | Amended: 1996, 2013
Governs residential leases and tenant-landlord relations. Sets minimum lease terms, notice periods for termination, and tenant protection against arbitrary eviction. Regulates rent increases for controlled accommodation. Applies to residential leases; commercial leases are regulated separately under the COC.
LandCivil

Co-ownership Law — Law No. 1977-42 of May 23, 1977Propriété par Étages ou par Appartements

Enacted: May 23, 1977 | In force: 1977 | Amended: 1994
Governs horizontal co-ownership (apartment buildings and condominiums). Defines common areas vs. private lots, establishes the mandatory Syndicate of Co-owners (syndicat des copropriétaires), and regulates management, maintenance costs, and dispute resolution between co-owners.

🏢 Associations & Civil Society

Associations Decree — Decree-Law No. 2011-88 of September 24, 2011

Enacted: September 24, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Replaced: Law No. 59-154 (1959)
Post-revolution statute liberalizing civil society formation. Replaced the restrictive 1959 law. Article 12: associations are legally constituted upon submission of a notification letter — no prior government approval required. Prohibits dissolution by executive action without judicial order. Enabled a rapid expansion of civil society: over 20,000 associations registered by 2015.
Key provisions
  • Art. 3: Freedom to form associations guaranteed
  • Art. 10: Founders must submit notification (not seek prior approval) to the Secretary General of Government
  • Art. 12: Association acquires legal personality upon notification
  • Art. 14: Registration certificate issued within 7 days
  • Art. 26: Dissolution only by judicial order on defined grounds
  • Art. 31: Foreign associations may operate with prior authorization
AdministrativeConstitutional

Political Parties Law — Decree-Law No. 2011-87 of September 24, 2011

Enacted: September 24, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Amended: 2014
Governs the formation, registration, financing, and activities of political parties. Requires notification-based registration (not prior authorization). Prohibits parties based on religion, race, gender, or region exclusively. Mandates financial transparency and annual reporting. Political party financing regulated: prohibits foreign funding, limits campaign spending.
AdministrativeConstitutional

Electoral Law — Organic Law No. 2014-16 of May 26, 2014(as amended)

Enacted: May 26, 2014 | In force: 2014 | Amended: 2019, 2022
Governs elections for the President of the Republic, the Assembly of People's Representatives, and (previously) the Assembly of Regions and Districts. Establishes the Independent High Authority for Elections (ISIE — Instance Supérieure Indépendante pour les Élections) as the permanent electoral commission. Sets rules on candidacy, campaigning, vote counting, and dispute resolution.

National Security Law — Law No. 2019-73 of December 31, 2019Internal Security Forces Statute

Enacted: December 31, 2019 | In force: 2020
Governs the organization, status, and conduct of Tunisia's internal security forces (National Police and National Guard). Defines missions, hierarchy, disciplinary procedures, and accountability mechanisms. Establishes an independent oversight body for police conduct complaints.

State of Emergency Decree — Decree No. 78-50 of January 26, 1978

Enacted: January 26, 1978 | In force: 1978
Establishes the legal framework for declaring and managing states of emergency. Grants expanded executive powers including restriction of movement, house arrest, administrative detention, and closure of meeting places. Tunisia declared a state of emergency in November 2015 (after Bardo and Sousse attacks), which was repeatedly extended and remained nominally in force for years.

Enacted: September 9, 1986 | In force: 1986 | Amended: 2019
Governs military offenses and the organization of military courts (tribunaux militaires). Military courts have jurisdiction over offenses by military personnel and certain offenses against national defense. A 2019 amendment restricted jurisdiction of military courts over civilians — a key human rights improvement.

🛂 Nationality, Immigration & Asylum

Entry and Residence of Foreigners — Law No. 68-7 of March 8, 1968

Enacted: March 8, 1968 | In force: 1968 | Amended: 1975, 2004
Governs the entry, stay, and expulsion of foreign nationals in Tunisia. Requires visa for most non-Maghreb nationals (bilateral visa exemptions apply). Establishes residence permit categories and procedures. Governs administrative detention pending deportation. Tunisia has no comprehensive asylum law; refugee status determined by UNHCR under its mandate.
NationalityAdministrative

Irregular Migration Law — Law No. 2004-6 of February 3, 2004

Enacted: February 3, 2004 | In force: 2004
Criminalizes irregular emigration by Tunisian nationals ("harraga") with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment. Criticized by human rights organizations as criminalizing movement. Used to prosecute irregular migrants attempting to cross to Europe. Tunisia also receives migrants from sub-Saharan Africa; their status is governed by the 1968 law and UNHCR procedure.

⚖️ Judicial Organization

Judicial Organization Law — Law No. 2013-13 of April 2, 2013

Enacted: April 2, 2013 | In force: 2013 | Amended: 2022
Organizes the court system of Tunisia. Courts of First Instance (Tribunaux de Première Instance) — one per governorate. Courts of Appeal — three (Tunis, Sousse, Sfax). Court of Cassation (Cour de Cassation) — apex civil and criminal court, based in Tunis. Administrative Court — apex administrative jurisdiction. Constitutional Court — established by 2022 Constitution. Cantonal courts handle minor civil and criminal matters.
Court structure
  • Cantonal Courts: minor civil matters, misdemeanor infractions
  • Courts of First Instance (24): civil, commercial, criminal chambers
  • Courts of Appeal (3 — Tunis, Sousse, Sfax): appellate jurisdiction
  • Court of Cassation (Tunis): reviews points of law only
  • Administrative Court: all public law disputes
  • Terrorism Chamber: specialized at TFI Tunis level
  • Constitutional Court: established under 2022 Constitution
AdministrativeConstitutional

Judicial Statute — Law No. 67-29 of July 14, 1967(as amended)

Enacted: July 14, 1967 | In force: 1967 | Amended: 1997, 2013, 2022
Governs the appointment, promotion, transfer, discipline, and removal of judges. Establishes the Superior Council of the Judiciary (Conseil Supérieur de la Magistrature — CSM) as the body responsible for judicial governance. Post-2022, the CSM's composition was changed, reducing independence according to critics.
AdministrativeConstitutional

Transitional Justice Law — Organic Law No. 2013-53 of December 24, 2013

Enacted: December 24, 2013 | In force: 2014
Establishes Tunisia's transitional justice framework including the Truth and Dignity Commission (IVD — Instance Vérité et Dignité). Covers: truth-seeking, reparations, institutional reform, and accountability for human rights violations committed between July 1, 1955 and December 31, 2013. IVD operated from 2014–2019; issued final report in 2019.

🌍 International Law & Key Treaties

Key International Conventions Ratified by Tunisia

Various dates
Tunisia is party to numerous international conventions that form part of Tunisian law upon ratification (monist approach).
Major conventions
  • UN Charter (1956) — upon independence
  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) — adopted, not treaty
  • International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights — ratified March 18, 1969
  • International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights — ratified March 18, 1969
  • Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) — ratified September 20, 1985 (with reservations; reservations partially lifted 2011)
  • Convention on the Rights of the Child — ratified January 29, 1992
  • UN Convention Against Torture — ratified September 23, 1988
  • African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights — ratified 1983
  • Arab Charter on Human Rights — ratified 2008
  • New York Convention on Arbitral Awards (1958) — ratified July 22, 1967
  • ILO Core Conventions (62 conventions ratified)
  • TRIPS Agreement — WTO member since March 29, 1995
  • WIPO Copyright Treaty — ratified 2012
  • Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) — member since March 1, 2001
  • UN Convention Against Corruption (UNCAC) — ratified October 23, 2008
  • Association Agreement with the European Union (1998)
Constitutional

EU-Tunisia Association Agreement — Law No. 97-37 of July 1, 1997

Signed: July 17, 1995 | Ratified by Tunisia: July 1, 1997 | In force: March 1, 1998
Association agreement between Tunisia and the European Union, the first such agreement with a Mediterranean country (Euro-Mediterranean Partnership). Established a free trade area for manufactured goods, progressively eliminating tariffs. Includes provisions on services, capital, intellectual property, and political dialogue. Tunisia achieved compliance with WTO rules under this agreement.
CommercialConstitutional

Arab Maghreb Union Treaty — 1989

Signed: February 17, 1989 (Marrakesh Treaty) | Ratified: 1989
Treaty establishing the Arab Maghreb Union (UMA) among Tunisia, Algeria, Morocco, Libya, and Mauritania. Aims for eventual economic integration. In practice, the union has been largely dormant due to Algeria-Morocco tensions. However, bilateral agreements within the AMU framework remain in force.
