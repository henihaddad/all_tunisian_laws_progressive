Tunisia's legal system is predominantly civil law-based, derived from the French legal tradition with elements of Islamic law (particularly in personal status). The unified secular judiciary was established in 1956 upon abolition of Shari'a courts. The current constitution is the 2022 Constitution (adopted July 25, 2022 by referendum; published August 18, 2022), which supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions. Official legal texts are published in the Journal Officiel de la République Tunisienne (JORT).

⚖️ Constitutional Law

Constitution of 2022Constitution of the Republic of Tunisia

Adopted: July 25, 2022 (referendum) | Published: August 18, 2022 | In force: August 2022
The current governing constitution, promulgated by Presidential Decree No. 2022-691 of August 17, 2022. Establishes a presidential system with a bicameral legislature (National Council of Regions and Districts + Assembly of People's Representatives). Supersedes the 1959 and 2014 constitutions as well as Decree-Law No. 2011-14 of March 23, 2011. Article 5 references Islam and "the aims of Pure Islam." Comprises 142 articles organized into 10 chapters.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam is its religion, Arabic its language, the republic its form of government
  • Art. 2: Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship, the will of the people, and the supremacy of law
  • Art. 5: The state shall work to achieve the objectives of Pure Islam
  • Art. 29: Intellectual property is guaranteed
  • Art. 40: Freedom to form political parties, unions, and associations is guaranteed
  • Art. 42: Freedom of peaceful assembly and demonstration is guaranteed
  • Art. 86–90: President elected for 5-year term, limit of two terms
  • Repeals: 1959 Constitution, Decree-Law No. 2011-14, 2014 Constitution
Update (2026): The Constitutional Court mandated by Art. 118 et seq. has still not been formed, four years after promulgation. A prior organic-law proposal collapsed in April 2025 when sponsoring deputies withdrew signatures; a new proposal (draft law n°100/2025, filed by 21 deputies) was still under committee hearings at the Assembly as of February 2026, with no enacted implementing law or appointed judges. See Pending Reforms.

Constitution of 2014 [Superseded]Constitution of the Republic of Tunisia

Adopted: January 26, 2014 | In force: February 2014 | Superseded: August 2022
Post-Arab Spring constitution adopted by the National Constituent Assembly with 200 votes for, 12 against, 4 abstentions. Comprised 149 articles across 10 chapters. Established a semi-presidential system. Article 74 restricted the presidency to Muslims. Now superseded by the 2022 Constitution.
Key provisions (historical)
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam its religion, Arabic its language
  • Art. 2: Tunisia is a civil state based on citizenship, the will of the people, and the primacy of law
  • Art. 20: Equality before the law guaranteed without discrimination
  • Art. 46: Enshrined women's rights and gender parity in elected bodies
  • Art. 74: Presidential candidates must be Muslim, Tunisian nationals, born of Tunisian parents
  • Art. 88: Mechanism for presidential impeachment
  • Art. 115: Prime minister removal required majority of assembly members

Constitution of 1959 [Superseded]Constitution of the Tunisian Republic

Promulgated: June 1, 1959 | In force: June 1, 1959 | Superseded: August 2022
Tunisia's first post-independence constitution. Modeled on the French system, vested strong executive power in a president. Arabic declared national language. Presidents elected for 5-year terms. Amended multiple times — notably in 2002 (removed presidential term limits, opened candidacy to persons over 75). In force for 63 years until replaced by the 2022 Constitution.
Key provisions (historical)
  • Art. 1: Tunisia is a free, independent, sovereign state; Islam its religion, Arabic its language, the republic its form
  • Art. 39: Presidential term set at 5 years
  • Art. 40: President elected by universal direct suffrage
  • 2002 amendment: removed two-term limit, raised age ceiling to 75

📜 Civil Law & Law of Obligations

Code of Obligations and Contracts (COC) — 1906Majallat Al-Iltizamat wa-l-Uqud

Promulgated: December 15, 1906 (under French Protectorate) | In force: January 1, 1907
The foundational civil code governing contracts, torts, and obligations. Derived from the French civil law tradition, adapted for Tunisian context. Covers general theory of obligations, specific contracts (sale, lease, agency, loan, deposit, surety), and civil liability. Has been substantially amended multiple times since independence. Remains in force as the core private law instrument.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–106: General theory of obligations (formation, validity, effects, extinction)
  • Art. 13: Requirements for valid contract — capacity, consent, object, cause
  • Art. 62–80: Nullity of contracts (absolute vs. relative)
  • Art. 82–96: Effects of obligations; performance and non-performance
  • Art. 100: Damages for non-performance; principle of full reparation
  • Art. 222–262: Contract of sale (conditions, effects, warranty)
  • Art. 728–733: Civil liability for delict and quasi-delict
  • Art. 786–828: Mandate and agency

Enacted: February 28, 1963 | In force: 1963 | Amended: 1975, 2002, 2010

👪 Personal Status Law

The Code of Personal Status (CPS) is Tunisia's most internationally distinctive statute — it abolishes polygamy and replaced unilateral repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce. It has been continuously in force since 1957.

Code of Personal Status (CPS) — Beylical Decree of August 13, 1956Majallat Al-Ahwal Al-Shakhsiyya

Enacted: August 13, 1956 (beylical decree under Lamine Bey) | In force: January 1, 1957 | Amended: 1959, 1966, 1981, 1993, 2017
Tunisia's landmark personal status code, principally drafted by President Habib Bourguiba. Abolished polygamy (criminal offense under Art. 18), replaced unilateral male repudiation (talaq) with court-supervised divorce, set minimum marriage age. The most progressive family law in the Arab world. Covers marriage, divorce, filiation, adoption (recognition of kafala), inheritance, guardianship, and wills. Never suspended or repealed despite political pressures (2011–2013). The 2022 Constitution has not altered its operation.
Key provisions
  • Art. 3: Marriage requires consent of both spouses; minimum age 18 (with exceptions)
  • Art. 5: Marriage must be registered and conducted before a civil status officer
  • Art. 18: Polygamy prohibited and criminalized — up to 1 year imprisonment + fine for marrying while already married
  • Art. 29–37: Divorce only by court order; grounds include mutual consent, harm, abandonment
  • Art. 31: Either spouse may initiate divorce; court-ordered alimony mandatory
  • Art. 46–67: Filiation and parentage; paternity presumption
  • Art. 68–78: Legal guardianship (tutelle) of minors
  • Art. 85–199: Inheritance rules (miras) — blends civil and Islamic principles
  • Art. 154–165: Guardianship over persons under disability
  • 1993 amendment: mother gains equal custody rights; divorced mothers may become guardians
  • 2017 amendment: allowed Muslim women to marry non-Muslim men (circular, not codified)

Enacted: November 9, 1995 | In force: 1995 | Amended: 2002, 2008
Comprehensive statute protecting minors from abuse, neglect, exploitation, and trafficking. Establishes the Children's Judge (juge des enfants) with jurisdiction over child protection cases. Enacted: March 4, 1958 | In force: 1958 | Amended: 1994
Regulates the kafala system — the legal guardianship of abandoned children (Tunisia does not recognize formal adoption in the Islamic sense). Allows Tunisian families to take in abandoned children as legal dependents. Creates rights and obligations equivalent to adoption without severing the child's original lineage.

Law No. 2017-58 of August 11, 2017Elimination of Violence Against Women

Enacted: August 11, 2017 | In force: February 2018
Landmark statute criminalizing all forms of violence against women — physical, psychological, sexual, and economic. Abolished the provision allowing rapists to escape prosecution by marrying their victim. Created specialized police units and prosecution procedures for gender-based violence. Established protective orders and victim shelters. Considered one of the most progressive anti-GBV laws in the Arab world.
Key provisions
  • Removed Art. 227 bis of the Penal Code (marriage escaping rape prosecution)
  • Defines and criminalizes economic, psychological, and sexual violence
  • Establishes specialized prosecution units for violence against women
  • Provides emergency protective orders for victims
  • Mandatory reporting obligations for health professionals
  • National Observatory on Violence Against Women created

⚔️ Criminal Law & Procedure

Penal Code — Law of July 9, 1913 (as amended)Majallat Al-Qanun Al-Jaza'i

Originally enacted: July 9, 1913 (French Protectorate) | Revised: 1964, 1968, 1987, 1993, 1999, 2004, 2010, 2017
Tunisia's criminal code. Enacted under the French Protectorate in 1913, substantially revised after independence. Classifies offenses into crimes (felonies), délits (misdemeanors), and contraventions (infractions). Organized in two books: general provisions and specific offenses. Notable provisions include criminalization of same-sex relations (Art. 230), blasphemy provisions, and offenses against state security. Amended significantly in 2017 following the Violence Against Women Law.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1–59: General principles — criminal liability, complicity, recidivism, penalties
  • Art. 52: Capital punishment available for certain crimes (murder, rape causing death, terrorism)
  • Art. 72–85: Offenses against state security (treason, espionage)
  • Art. 86: Attacks against the head of state or government
  • Art. 121: Prohibited associations
  • Art. 214: Abortion prohibited except to save mother's life
  • Art. 218: Homicide and serious assault
  • Art. 226: Offenses against public morals
  • Art. 226 bis: Defamation of public officials via new communication technologies
  • Art. 227: Rape — minimum 6 years imprisonment
  • Art. 230: Consensual same-sex relations criminalized — up to 3 years imprisonment
  • Art. 231–245: Theft, fraud, embezzlement
  • Art. 305: Receiving stolen goods

Code of Criminal Procedure — Law No. 68-23 of July 24, 1968

Enacted: July 24, 1968 | Published: JORT No. 32 (August 2–6, 1968) | Amended: Law No. 87-70 (Nov. 26, 1987), Law No. 93-114 (Nov. 22, 1993)
Governs all stages of criminal proceedings in Tunisia. Organized in five books covering: public action and preliminary investigation, trial court jurisdictions, extraordinary remedies (appeals, cassation), special procedures, and execution of sentences. Article 1 establishes that any infraction gives rise to public action aimed at applying penalties.
Key provisions
  • Art. 1: Any infraction triggers public action ("Toute infraction donne ouverture à une action publique")
  • Art. 13–14 (as amended by Law No. 87-70): Limits on garde à vue detention
  • Art. 47: Preliminary judicial investigation mandatory for crimes (felonies)
  • Art. 57: Rights of suspects during investigation
  • Art. 85–87 (as amended by Law No. 93-114): Preventive detention — initial 6-month maximum, extendable
  • Art. 106–107: Extended detention provisions
  • Book II: Jurisdiction of cantonal courts, courts of first instance, courts of appeal
  • Book III: Opposition, appeal, and cassation remedies
  • Book IV: Military courts, terrorism courts, special jurisdictions
  • Book V: Execution of criminal sentences

Anti-Terrorism Law — Law No. 2015-26 of July 7, 2015

Enacted: July 7, 2015 | In force: July 2015
Comprehensive counter-terrorism statute enacted following the Bardo Museum and Sousse beach attacks (2015). Replaced the 2003 anti-terrorism law. Defines terrorism broadly, criminalizes financing, propaganda, recruitment, and travel for terrorist purposes. Establishes specialized anti-terrorism courts and a national anti-terrorism agency (CTAF). Criticized by human rights groups for overly broad definitions.
Key provisions
  • Broad definition of "terrorist act" including threats to public order
  • Criminalization of glorification of terrorism and incitement
  • Criminalizes financing of terrorism and related financial flows
  • Prohibition on travel to conflict zones for terrorist purposes
  • Specialized Terrorism Chamber at the Court of First Instance of Tunis
  • Extended detention periods for terrorism suspects
  • Asset freezing without prior judicial authorization

Organic Law No. 2017-10 of March 7, 2017Reporting of Corruption and Protection of Whistleblowers

Enacted: March 7, 2017 | Published: JORT 2017-020 | In force: 2017
Establishes the legal procedure for reporting corruption in the public and private sectors and creates protections for whistleblowers (lanceurs d'alerte) who report in good faith, including identity confidentiality and — per Government Decrees No. 2019-1123 and 2019-1124 — security protection and financial rewards. Reports were historically channeled through INLUCC, the independent anti-corruption body created by Framework Decree-Law No. 2011-120 of November 14, 2011. INLUCC was shut down by security forces in July–August 2021 as part of President Saied's emergency measures; a temporary head was installed by presidential decree in October 2022, but INLUCC's case files and independent functioning remain unresolved. The 2022 Constitution removed the constitutional chapter on independent bodies altogether, leaving Tunisia without a fully functioning, independent anti-corruption authority as of 2026 — no verified decree has formally re-established or renamed INLUCC. As of December 2024, the Interior Ministry reported extending security protection to over 50 whistleblowers under this law.

Law No. 2018-46 of August 1, 2018Declaration of Assets and Interests; Illicit Enrichment and Conflicts of Interest

Adopted by ARP: July 2018 (126 votes in favor) | Enacted: August 1, 2018
Requires elected officials, senior public servants, magistrates, and other listed categories of public office-holders to declare their assets and interests — in Tunisia and abroad, including those of spouses and minor children — within 60 days of taking office or of final election results. Declarations have two parts: an asset declaration and a declaration of interests, aimed at preventing conflicts of interest and detecting illicit enrichment. Over 350,000 individuals were estimated to fall within scope at adoption. Distinct from, but complementary to, the whistleblower-protection statute above (Organic Law 2017-10); both were historically administered through INLUCC.

Organic Law No. 2018-50 of October 23, 2018Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination

Adopted by ARP: October 9, 2018 | Published: October 23, 2018 | In force: 2018
The first law in the Middle East and North Africa to criminalize racial discrimination. Defines racial discrimination as any distinction, exclusion, restriction, or preference based on race, color, descent, or national or ethnic origin that impairs equal enjoyment of rights and freedoms. Drafted with civil-society groups ADAM for Equality and Development and Mnemty following high-profile incidents of racial violence in 2016–2018. Criminalizes racist statements/hate speech (1 month–1 year imprisonment, fine up to ~1,000 TND) and incitement or organized racist acts (1–3 years imprisonment, fine up to ~3,000 TND). Implementation has been uneven — civil-society reviews describe "mixed results" — but the law has been actively used: 14 cases involving 18 defendants were heard in 2024. In November 2025, the UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination commended Tunisia's removal of restrictions on registering Amazigh names while raising continued concerns over protections for migrants and hate speech enforcement.

Law No. 92-52 of May 18, 1992Narcotics and Psychotropic Substances

Enacted: May 18, 1992 | In force: 1992 | Amended: 1999, 2017
Governs narcotics and psychotropic substances including cultivation, production, trafficking, possession, and use. Imposes severe penalties — mandatory minimum 1 year for consumption (Art. 4), up to life imprisonment for trafficking. Article 4 on personal drug use attracted international criticism; the 2017 amendment introduced some discretion for judges in consumption cases.
Key provisions
  • Art. 4: Personal consumption of narcotics — 1 to 5 years imprisonment + fine (mandatory)
  • Art. 5: Trafficking — 6 years to life imprisonment
  • Art. 12: Confiscation of proceeds and instruments of offenses
  • 2017 amendment: judges given discretion on sentencing for first-time consumption offenses

Decree-Law No. 2022-54 of September 13, 2022Combating Offenses Related to Information and Communication Systems

Enacted: September 13, 2022 | In force: September 2022 | No formal amendment as of 2026
Criminalizes use of telecom networks to produce or spread "false news" or rumors that harm third parties, public order, or national defense (Art. 24: up to 5 years' imprisonment and a TND 50,000 fine, doubled where the target is a public official). Criticized by UN Special Rapporteurs (January 2023), Amnesty International, ICJ, ARTICLE 19, and the National Syndicate of Tunisian Journalists (SNJT) for vague language enabling prosecution of journalists, lawyers, and activists. Parliamentary attempts to revise it (2023, 2025) were both blocked. Human Rights Watch documented at least 22 prosecutions of journalists/activists by December 2023; by 2024–2026 the Committee to Protect Journalists recorded the highest count of jailed journalists in Tunisia since it began tracking in 1992, including Borhen Bsaies, Mourad Zeghidi, Sonia Dahmani, Ramlah Dahmani, Ghassen Ben Khelifa, and Zied el-Heni. From 2026, authorities have increasingly paired Decree-54 charges with financial-crime allegations and administrative measures (asset freezes, dissolution proceedings) against independent outlets.

Law No. 2024-41 of August 2, 2024Reform of Bounced/Dishonored Cheques

Adopted by ARP: July 30, 2024 | Published: JORT No. 94, August 2, 2024 | Entry into force: February 3, 2025
Major reform of Commercial Code provisions on cheques, enacted after sustained civil-society pressure (Human Rights Watch's June 2024 "No Way Out" report documented hundreds imprisoned over unpaid debts). Amends Articles 410, 410 bis–sexies, 411, 411 quinquies, 411 septies, 412, 412 ter–quater, and 732 of the Commercial Code. Introduced the "TuniChèque" digital verification platform. Central Bank data show a roughly 94% drop in daily cheque transactions in the year following the reform, with a corresponding surge in cash circulation.
Key provisions
  • Cheques ≤ 5,000 TND: prosecution for insufficient funds now requires a complaint from the beneficiary rather than automatic ex officio prosecution
  • Art. 411 (new): for cheques > 5,000 TND, penalty reduced to 2 years' imprisonment + fine of 20% of the shortfall (down from up to 5 years)
  • Bearer cheques abolished — all cheques must name a payee; endorsement limited to bank deposit/collection
  • New standardized cheque with QR code; per-cheque cap of 30,000 TND above which certified cheques are required
  • Banks must verify client solvency and set per-client ceilings before issuing chequebooks (BCT Circular No. 2024-14)
  • Art. 412 ter: caps interest rates on long-term loans (>7 years, after 3 years repaid) where the rate exceeds 8% of remaining capital
  • Transitional amnesty/regularization mechanisms for persons already prosecuted or imprisoned over dishonored cheques issued before publication

🏢 Associations & Civil Society

Associations Decree — Decree-Law No. 2011-88 of September 24, 2011

Enacted: September 24, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Replaced: Law No. 59-154 (1959)
Post-revolution statute liberalizing civil society formation. Replaced the restrictive 1959 law. Article 12: associations are legally constituted upon submission of a notification letter — no prior government approval required. Prohibits dissolution by executive action without judicial order. Enabled a rapid expansion of civil society: over 20,000 associations registered by 2015.
Key provisions
  • Art. 3: Freedom to form associations guaranteed
  • Art. 10: Founders must submit notification (not seek prior approval) to the Secretary General of Government
  • Art. 12: Association acquires legal personality upon notification
  • Art. 14: Registration certificate issued within 7 days
  • Art. 26: Dissolution only by judicial order on defined grounds
  • Art. 31: Foreign associations may operate with prior authorization

Political Parties Law — Decree-Law No. 2011-87 of September 24, 2011

Enacted: September 24, 2011 | In force: 2011 | Amended: 2014
Governs the formation, registration, financing, and activities of political parties. Requires notification-based registration (not prior authorization). Prohibits parties based on religion, race, gender, or region exclusively. Mandates financial transparency and annual reporting. Electoral Law — Organic Law No. 2014-16 of May 26, 2014(as amended)

Enacted: May 26, 2014 | In force: 2014 | Amended: 2019, 2022 (x2), 2023, 2024
Governs elections for the President of the Republic and the Assembly of People's Representatives. Establishes the Independent High Authority for Elections (ISIE) as the permanent electoral commission. Sets rules on candidacy, campaigning, vote counting, and dispute resolution. Substantially reshaped since 2022 by the decree-laws below, which moved from party-list to individual candidacy, placed ISIE under presidential appointment, and — most significantly — stripped the Administrative Court of jurisdiction over election disputes shortly before the 2024 presidential vote.

Decree-Law No. 2022-22 of April 21, 2022ISIE Governance Reform

Enacted: April 21, 2022 | In force: 2022
Amended Organic Law No. 2012-23 governing ISIE. Replaced parliamentary election of ISIE's seven board members (previously requiring a 145-vote supermajority) with a presidential-appointment model: 3 members chosen directly by the President from former ISIE members, 3 magistrates proposed by judicial bodies, and 1 engineer, each confirmed by presidential decree for a non-renewable 4-year term. ISIE's then-president described the authority as having "collapsed" in independence terms.

Decree-Law No. 2022-55 of September 15, 2022Individual Candidacy Electoral Reform

Enacted: September 15, 2022 | In force: 2022
Replaced closed party-list proportional representation for parliamentary elections with an individual candidacy system: candidates run individually and must collect 400 voter signatures (50% women, 25% under-35), may stand in only one of 161 electoral districts (10 reserved for Tunisians abroad), and campaigns are self-financed after the repeal of public campaign financing — removing the gender-parity safeguard that the former list system provided.

Decree-Law No. 2023-8 of March 8, 2023Municipal/Local Elections Amendment

Enacted: March 8, 2023 | In force: 2023
Amended Organic Law No. 2014-16 for local council elections: candidates must be residents registered in the district where they run; each municipality became an automatic electoral constituency; limited-choice ballot rules applied (voters select up to the council's seat count, with no erasures or additions). Applied to the December 2023 local council elections. A companion Decree-Law No. 2023-10 (same date) organized regional council composition.

Organic Law No. 2024-45 of September 28, 2024Transfer of Electoral Dispute Jurisdiction

Adopted by ARP: September 27, 2024 (116–12–8) | Published: JORT, September 28, 2024 | In force: immediately, days before the 2024 presidential election
Stripped the Administrative Court of jurisdiction over electoral disputes and transferred it to the Tunis Court of Appeal (with appeal to the Court of Cassation), with a 48-hour filing window. Article 4 applied retroactively to pending disputes — directly reversing an August 2024 Administrative Court ruling that had ordered ISIE to reinstate three disqualified presidential candidates (Mondher Zenaidi, Abdellatif Mekki, Imed Daïmi), a ruling ISIE had refused to implement. Passed and signed within hours, roughly ten days before the vote; condemned by HRW, ICJ, and civil society as unconstitutional, since the 2022 Constitution states Administrative Court rulings are not subject to appeal.

2024 Presidential Election

Vote: October 6, 2024 | Results announced: October 7, 2024 | Inauguration: October 21, 2024
First presidential election held under the 2022 Constitution and the amended electoral framework above. Only 3 of many aspirants were approved to run — incumbent Kais Saied, Ayachi Zammel (imprisoned during much of the campaign), and Zouhair Maghzaoui — after ISIE disregarded the Administrative Court's reinstatement order for three other candidates. 🛂 Nationality, Immigration & Asylum

Entry and Residence of Foreigners — Law No. 68-7 of March 8, 1968

Enacted: March 8, 1968 | In force: 1968 | Amended: 1975, 2004
Governs the entry, stay, and expulsion of foreign nationals in Tunisia. Requires visa for most non-Maghreb nationals (bilateral visa exemptions apply). Establishes residence permit categories and procedures. Governs administrative detention pending deportation. Tunisia has no comprehensive asylum law; refugee status determined by UNHCR under its mandate.
NationalityAdministrative

Irregular Migration Law — Law No. 2004-6 of February 3, 2004

Enacted: February 3, 2004 | In force: 2004
Criminalizes irregular emigration by Tunisian nationals ("harraga") with penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment. Criticized by human rights organizations as criminalizing movement. Used to prosecute irregular migrants attempting to cross to Europe. Tunisia also receives migrants from sub-Saharan Africa; their status is governed by the 1968 law and UNHCR procedure, which has been suspended since June 2024 (see below).
NationalityCriminal

2023–2026 Sub-Saharan Migrant Policy Shift

Presidential statement: February 21, 2023 | UNHCR suspension: June 2024 | Ongoing as of 2026
Following a February 21, 2023 statement by President Saied describing irregular sub-Saharan migration as part of a "criminal plan" to alter Tunisia's demographic composition, authorities enforced existing law (No. 68-7 of 1968 and general criminal law — no new decree was issued) far more aggressively, with reports of mass evictions, arbitrary arrests, and expulsions to the Algerian and Libyan desert borders. In June 2024, authorities ordered UNHCR to suspend refugee registration and status-determination activities — the only functioning avenue for asylum in the absence of a national asylum law — a suspension still in effect as of 2026. A May 2024 bill sharply raising penalties under Law 68-7 (imprisonment from 1–3 years, fines to TND 5,000) remains in parliamentary committee, unconfirmed as adopted. Tunisia has never enacted a comprehensive asylum law.

EU–Tunisia Memorandum of UnderstandingStrategic and Global Partnership

Signed: July 16, 2023
Non-binding political declaration between the EU and Tunisia covering five pillars: macroeconomic stability, trade/investment, green energy, people-to-people contacts, and — most emphasized — migration and mobility. The EU pledged €105 million for border-control capacity, €150 million in direct budget support, and a conditional €900 million in macro-financial assistance tied to an IMF program Tunisia ultimately declined. Tunisia committed to combating smuggling networks, conducting search-and-rescue in its waters, and accepting returns of third-country nationals. Bypassed parliamentary ratification on both sides. In January 2025 the European Commission overhauled its disbursement mechanism following reports of abuses by EU-funded security forces; Tunisian officials have since pushed to renegotiate the deal toward greater reciprocity.

⚖️ Judicial Organization

Judicial Organization Law — Law No. 2013-13 of April 2, 2013

Enacted: April 2, 2013 | In force: 2013 | Amended: 2022
Organizes the court system of Tunisia. Courts of First Instance (Tribunaux de Première Instance) — one per governorate. Courts of Appeal — three (Tunis, Sousse, Sfax). Court of Cassation (Cour de Cassation) — apex civil and criminal court, based in Tunis. Administrative Court — apex administrative jurisdiction. Constitutional Court — established by 2022 Constitution. Cantonal courts handle minor civil and criminal matters.
Court structure
  • Cantonal Courts: minor civil matters, misdemeanor infractions
  • Courts of First Instance (24): civil, commercial, criminal chambers
  • Courts of Appeal (3 — Tunis, Sousse, Sfax): appellate jurisdiction
  • Court of Cassation (Tunis): reviews points of law only
  • Administrative Court: all public law disputes
  • Terrorism Chamber: specialized at TFI Tunis level
  • Constitutional Court: established under 2022 Constitution
AdministrativeConstitutional

Judicial Statute — Law No. 67-29 of July 14, 1967(as amended)

Enacted: July 14, 1967 | In force: 1967 | Amended: 1997, 2013, 2022
Governs the appointment, promotion, transfer, discipline, and removal of judges. Historically implemented through the elected Superior Council of the Judiciary (CSM), replaced in 2022 by the decree-laws below. In May–June 2026, the Tunisian National Bar Association (ONAT) staged an escalating protest campaign — including rallies at courts in Tunis, Bizerte, Béja, Jendouba, Le Kef, and Siliana — culminating in a nationwide lawyers' general strike on June 18, 2026 demanding reform of the legal framework governing the profession and stronger judicial independence guarantees. The strike followed a May 1, 2026 ONAT extraordinary general assembly whose decisions the Tunis public prosecutor sought to have judicially nullified. As of early July 2026 the Justice Ministry had given no formal response and ONAT had not announced further escalation.

Decree-Law No. 2022-11 of February 12, 2022Temporary Supreme Judicial Council

Enacted: February 12, 2022 | In force: 2022
Dissolved the elected Conseil Supérieur de la Magistrature (established under the 2014 Constitution and Organic Law No. 2016-34, which it repealed) and replaced it with a provisional council roughly half of whose members are directly appointed by the President. Grants the President power to object to judicial appointments and control postings/promotions/transfers; bans judges from striking. President Saied justified the move by accusing the former CSM of "selling positions." The International Commission of Jurists called the decree "unconstitutional and illegal."

Decree-Law No. 2022-35 of June 1, 2022Dismissal of 57 Judges

Enacted: June 1, 2022 | In force: June 1, 2022
Distinct from Decree-Law No. 2022-11, this instrument empowered the President to dismiss judges directly and was used the same day to fire 57 judges on accusations including obstruction of terrorism investigations and corruption; dismissal automatically triggers criminal prosecution, appealable only after the criminal case concludes. In August 2022, the Tunis Administrative Court ordered reinstatement of 49 of the 57 judges, finding violations of fair-trial rights, presumption of innocence, and rights of defense; the Ministry of Justice did not comply. In January 2023, 37 of the dismissed judges filed criminal complaints against the Justice Minister for non-compliance with the court order.

Underlying incident: 2022 magistrates' strike | Appeals ruling: July 1, 2026
The Tunis Court of Appeal's criminal chamber confirmed the conviction of Anas Hmaidi, president of the Association of Tunisian Magistrates (AMT), on charges of "obstructing freedom of work" arising from his role interrupting a 2022 court hearing during the judges' protest movement that followed the mass dismissals under Decree-Law No. 2022-35. The appeals court increased his sentence from a one-year suspended term to one year of firm imprisonment with immediate execution; Hmaidi did not appear at the hearing. The ruling lands amid the broader 2022–2026 pattern of prosecutions of judges, lawyers, and magistrates' representatives connected to the post-2022 restructuring of judicial governance.
